Frequently Asked Questions

What is QPIN Private Limited?
QPIN is India’s quantum trailblazer, established under the Ministry of Science and Technology to build cutting-edge quantum processors. Our ₹369 crore 100-qubit project by 2026 is just the start—unlocking solutions for society, from secure banking to sustainable energy.
What is the 100-qubit quantum processor project?
Launched June 2, 2025, this ₹369 crore initiative delivers a 100-qubit processor by December 2026. It’s faster than supercomputers for specific tasks—like factoring numbers in minutes—paving the way for breakthroughs in cryptography and medicine, benefiting everyone.
How does QPIN collaborate with others?
We partner with IITs, Oxford Instruments, and AWS to blend expertise and innovation. These collaborations mean faster tech for India—like secure data and optimized transport—making life better for millions.
What’s next after the 100-qubit processor?
We’re scaling to 1000 qubits by 2035 and 100,000 by 2045, solving grand challenges like climate modeling. This means cleaner air and smarter cities for future generations.
How can I join QPIN’s team?

Why is India investing in quantum tech?
India aims to lead the global tech race, solving national challenges like cybersecurity and healthcare. Quantum tech means safer digital transactions and faster drug discovery—lifting millions out of poverty and illness.
How does QPIN fit into India’s National Quantum Mission?
QPIN is a key player in India’s NQM, launched in 2023 with ₹6000 crore. Our 100-qubit project accelerates this vision, creating jobs and tech that protect and prosper society.
Will quantum computing create jobs in India?
Yes! We’re training 100,000 quantum experts by 2045 via QII and Quantum Corps. These jobs—from coding to research—will boost India’s economy and empower youth.
How will India use quantum tech for society?
From optimizing Railways (saving ₹500 crore by 2030) to modeling monsoons (better farming by 2040), quantum tech will improve infrastructure and food security for all Indians.
Can India rival global quantum leaders?
Absolutely. With ₹369 crore, we match IBM’s ₹900 crore efficiency. By 2045, hosting the World Quantum Summit, India will lead, sharing solutions globally.
What’s the global state of quantum computing in 2025?
IBM’s at 1,121 qubits (Condor), Google’s pushing fault-tolerance. QPIN’s 100 qubits join this race, offering affordable innovation for societal good—like cheaper medicine.
How does quantum computing differ from classical?
Quantum uses qubits—superposition and entanglement—to solve problems exponentially faster. This means curing diseases or optimizing energy grids in ways classical PCs can’t, benefiting humanity.
What’s the world’s quantum goal by 2035?
Global leaders aim for fault-tolerant systems (10⁶ qubits). QPIN’s 1000-qubit target aligns here, promising secure communications and climate solutions for a safer planet.
Will quantum computing break encryption?
Yes, by 2033 (QPIN’s 500 qubits), Shor’s algorithm could crack AES. But it also drives post-quantum cryptography, keeping your data safe in a digital world.
How will quantum tech impact climate change?
By 2040, QPIN and others will model climate with 10,000 qubits, optimizing renewables and cutting emissions—giving society a fighting chance against global warming.
What’s the biggest quantum challenge globally?
Error correction—reducing noise below 10⁻⁶. QPIN’s tackling this by 2033, ensuring reliable tech for healthcare and logistics improvements worldwide.
How does quantum computing help AI?
By 2036, QPIN’s 1000 qubits will speed up AI training (e.g., quantum neural nets), enhancing diagnostics and smart cities—making life healthier and easier.
Who are the global quantum leaders?
IBM, Google, and China lead now. QPIN’s cost-efficient 100-qubit leap shows India—and society—can benefit from this race without breaking the bank.
What’s the ultimate quantum dream?
By 2045, millions of qubits could simulate reality itself—solving hunger with optimized agriculture or curing cancer with perfect drugs. QPIN’s part of that dream for all.
